Topic 1 · Zero-dose burden

Estimating and mapping zero-dose children

Core policy question

Which woredas have the highest zero-dose prevalence and counts, and how uncertain are those estimates?

Why it matters

Survey estimates are strong nationally and regionally; routine data add local detail but require careful treatment.

Primary user

Immunization programme teams setting equity-focused woreda priorities and allocating resources.

Topic 1 · Data and prototype

Zero-dose burden mapping

Available data

  • EDHS 2000, 2005, 2011 and 2016; Mini-EDHS 2019 vaccination histories.
  • Routine Penta1/DTP1 reporting, with reporting completeness.
  • Under-one population, facilities, urban–rural class and travel time.
  • Harmonized woreda boundaries.

Prototype direction

  • Estimate prevalence and counts by woreda.
  • Rank priority woredas with uncertainty intervals.
  • Produce a map and table for microplanning.
  • Document assumptions, gaps and interpretation limits.

Design target: credible enough for review and simple enough to explain.

Topic 2 · Malaria allocation

Forecasting and allocating medicines

Core policy question

How should available antimalarial medicines be allocated for the next replenishment cycle to reduce shortages, excess stock and inequity?

Why it matters

Burden changes across place and season, while observed consumption can understate need during stock-outs.

Primary user

Malaria programme and supply teams planning replenishment under a limited available supply.

Topic 2 · Data and prototype

Forecasting and constrained allocation

Available data

  • Malaria surveillance, testing, reporting and seasonality.
  • Medicine issues, consumption, usable stock and pipeline stock.
  • Approved treatment and buffer-stock parameters.
  • Population and service-capacity data.

Prototype direction

  • Forecast cases and product-specific treatment requirements.
  • Estimate likely shortages and excess stock.
  • Compare current, burden-based and equity-weighted scenarios.
  • Recommend a constrained allocation for the agreed cycle.

Design target: credible enough for review and simple enough to explain.

Topic 3 · Measles alert rules

Evaluating thresholds for early detection

Core policy question

Which alert rules detect measles events earlier while avoiding unnecessary false alerts?

Why it matters

Fixed thresholds may not reflect population size, baseline incidence or surveillance sensitivity.

Primary user

Surveillance and immunization teams applying consistent rules in routine monitoring.

Topic 3 · Data and prototype

Measles alert threshold evaluation

Available data

  • At least five years of case-based and weekly surveillance, where available.
  • Laboratory and outbreak-investigation records.
  • Routine immunization and campaign data.
  • Population, geography and approved surveillance guidance.

Prototype direction

  • Reconstruct current and alternative alerts.
  • Compare sensitivity, timeliness and positive predictive value.
  • Measure false-alert burden and investigation workload.
  • Recommend a practical rule and the action after an alert.

Design target: credible enough for review and simple enough to explain.

Topic 4 · Measles transmission

Comparing control and elimination pathways

Core policy question

Which combination of routine immunization, catch-up, SIAs and outbreak response is most likely to reduce burden and interrupt sustained transmission?

Why it matters

Transmission reflects vaccination coverage, births, age structure and response timing.

Primary user

Immunization and disease-prevention teams comparing coverage, catch-up and response strategies.

Topic 4 · Data and prototype

Transmission and elimination modelling

Available data

  • Measles surveillance by age, time and location.
  • MCV1, MCV2 and SIA coverage over recent years.
  • Population size, births, mortality and age structure.
  • Biological parameters and age-specific contact patterns.

Prototype direction

  • Reconstruct age- and time-specific susceptibility.
  • Build a model with an observation process for reported cases.
  • Calibrate on an earlier period and validate later patterns.
  • Compare routine, catch-up, SIA and response scenarios.

Design target: credible enough for review and simple enough to explain.

Before family formation

Questions to clarify

  • Which data are available now?
  • Which gaps or biases must be stated?
  • What method is realistic in six days?
  • What prototype will be demonstrated?
  • What message should reach programme leaders?
